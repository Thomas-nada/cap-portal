# What if a CAP Editor disagrees with my proposal?

Editors do not have veto power over proposals based on policy disagreement. Their role is to assess completeness, constitutionality, and readiness — not whether they personally agree with the substance of the change.

---

## What an Editor can and cannot do

**An Editor can:**
- Ask clarifying questions about your intent or reasoning
- Flag a constitutional problem — for example, if the proposal appears to conflict with an existing Article or Guardrail
- Note that the proposal is incomplete and decline to move it to the Ready stage until it is ready
- Suggest edits to improve clarity or structure

**An Editor cannot:**
- Change your proposal without your consent
- Reject a proposal because they disagree with its policy direction
- Permanently block a proposal from proceeding

---

## If an Editor raises a constitutional concern

Take the concern seriously — if the Editor has identified a genuine conflict with the Constitution, the governance action would likely be blocked by the CC at the on-chain stage anyway. It is worth addressing constitutional issues in the off-chain process.

However, you are not required to accept the Editor's framing. You may respond in the comment thread, explain your reasoning, and let the community weigh in. The deliberation thread is the place for that debate.

---

## If you fundamentally disagree with an Editor's approach

Authors retain full ownership of their proposals. If you believe an Editor is acting outside their remit or blocking a proposal on improper grounds, you may:

- Raise the issue in the public comment thread so the community and other Editors can respond
- Attend or submit to a CAP Editor public meeting (recorded in `CAP-Editors/CAPSLOCK/`)
- Bypass the CAP process entirely and submit a governance action on-chain directly

The CAP process is not a gatekeeping mechanism. No Editor can prevent you from submitting a governance action to the chain.

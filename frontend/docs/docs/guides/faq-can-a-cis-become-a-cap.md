# Can a CIS become a CAP?

Not directly — a CIS does not transform into a CAP. But a CIS is often the starting point that leads to one or more CAPs being submitted.

---

## The intended relationship

A CIS defines a problem. A CAP proposes a solution.

The normal sequence is:

1. Someone identifies a constitutional ambiguity, gap, or risk and submits a **CIS** to document it
2. The community deliberates on the CIS — discussing the nature of the problem and whether it needs addressing
3. One or more authors submit a **CAP** (or multiple competing CAPs) proposing how to resolve the issue raised by the CIS
4. The CAP(s) go through their own deliberation and, if accepted, proceed to the on-chain stage

The CIS and the CAP are separate issues in GitHub. The CAP should reference the CIS in its Motivation section to show which documented problem it is addressing.

---

## Can I submit both at once?

Yes. If you have already identified the problem and have a clear solution in mind, you may submit a CAP directly without submitting a CIS first. The CIS step is valuable when the problem itself needs community alignment before solutions are debated — it is not mandatory.

---

## What happens to the CIS after a CAP is ratified?

The CIS remains in the repository as a closed issue once the problem it identified has been addressed. It serves as part of the governance history — a record of why a change was made.

If the CAP only partially resolves the CIS, or if the ratified change raises new questions, the CIS may remain open for continued discussion, or a new CIS may be submitted.

---

## Can one CIS lead to multiple CAPs?

Yes, and this is expected. A CIS that identifies a broad governance gap may inspire several CAPs with different approaches to the same problem. The community can deliberate on competing proposals simultaneously and ultimately decide which, if any, to advance.

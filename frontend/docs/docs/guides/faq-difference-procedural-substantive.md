# What is the difference between Procedural and Substantive?

Both categories carry a 60-day recommended minimum deliberation period, but they describe different kinds of changes. Choosing the right one matters for accurately representing what your proposal does.

---

## Procedural

A **Procedural** CAP changes how governance works — the rules and processes that govern participation, rather than the rights or principles themselves.

If your proposal answers the question *"how is this done?"*, it is likely Procedural.

**Examples:**
- Changing how CAP Editors are appointed or removed
- Modifying the recommended minimum deliberation periods themselves
- Altering the format or structure of the CAP submission process
- Changing the process by which CC elections are held

---

## Substantive

A **Substantive** CAP changes what the Constitution actually says and protects — rights, obligations, principles, or the powers of governance bodies.

If your proposal answers the question *"what is permitted, required, or protected?"*, it is likely Substantive.

**Examples:**
- Adding or removing a Tenet
- Changing the rights enumerated in Article II (ada owner rights)
- Altering the voting threshold for constitutional amendments
- Expanding or restricting the CC's scope of review
- Modifying the Treasury Withdrawals standards

---

## When the line is blurry

Some proposals affect both process and substance. For example, a proposal that changes how the CC is elected (Procedural) might also change the criteria for CC membership (Substantive).

When a proposal spans both categories, choose the one that best reflects the primary effect of the change. You can also note the dual nature in your Abstract or Rationale. A CAP Editor may suggest a different category during initial review if the classification seems off.

---

## Does the category affect the outcome?

The category affects the recommended minimum deliberation period and signals the significance of the change to reviewers and the community. Mislabelling a Substantive change as Editorial or Interpretive — even unintentionally — is one of the common mistakes that Editors flag early.

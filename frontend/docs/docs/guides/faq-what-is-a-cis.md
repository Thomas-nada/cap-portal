# What is a CIS?

A **Constitutional Issue Statement (CIS)** is a formal document that defines a constitutional problem — an ambiguity, procedural gap, or governance risk — without proposing a specific solution.

---

## The short version

A CIS says: "something in the Constitution is unclear, incomplete, or broken — here is the problem." It does not propose a fix. That is left to a CAP.

---

## When to use a CIS instead of a CAP

Use a CIS when:

- You have spotted an ambiguity in the constitutional text but aren't certain how it should be resolved
- You want to document a governance gap and open it for community discussion before proposing a solution
- You want to establish a shared understanding of a problem before debating solutions

If you already have a specific proposed change in mind, you can skip directly to a CAP. But if the community hasn't agreed that a problem exists, a CIS is often the better first step.

---

## What a CIS contains

A CIS identifies:

- The specific provision(s) affected
- What the ambiguity, gap, or risk is
- Why it matters — the governance implications of leaving it unresolved

It does **not** include a proposed amendment. Proposing text is the job of a CAP.

---

## How CISs and CAPs relate

A single CIS may inspire multiple CAPs with different approaches to the same problem. A single CAP may resolve multiple CISs. Neither depends on the other — both can be submitted independently.

Even without a corresponding CAP, a CIS has value: it creates a shared, documented record of a governance challenge that the community and editors can reference.

---

## Submitting a CIS

CISs are submitted as GitHub Issues using the same form as CAPs, with the type set to **CIS**. The deliberation period is 30 days for most CISs. See [Creating a CAP or CIS via GitHub](creating-a-cap) for step-by-step instructions.
